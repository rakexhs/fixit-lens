## Microwave capacitor

Microwave ovens contain a high-voltage capacitor that can store a lethal electrical charge, even for a long time after the microwave has been unplugged. Discharging it requires specific insulated tools and technique. Never open a microwave's outer case or touch internal components. If a microwave is sparking, arcing, or making unusual noises, unplug it (from the plug, not by touching the body) and contact a qualified appliance technician.

## CRT discharge

Cathode ray tube (CRT) televisions and monitors store extremely high voltage in the tube and flyback circuit long after being unplugged. Attempting to discharge a CRT without proper training and insulated tools can cause severe or fatal electric shock. Do not open a CRT device. Contact a qualified electronics technician or e-waste recycler.

## Gas appliance repair

Any repair involving a gas line, gas valve, gas stove, gas water heater, or gas furnace carries a risk of gas leak, fire, or explosion. Do not attempt to disconnect, repair, or modify gas fittings or valves yourself. If you smell gas, leave the area immediately, do not operate light switches or anything electrical, and contact your gas utility or emergency services from outside the building.

## Refrigerant handling

Refrigerant used in refrigerators, air conditioners, and heat pumps is pressurized and can cause frostbite, chemical exposure, or environmental harm if released improperly, and in most regions handling refrigerant legally requires certification. Do not attempt to open sealed refrigerant lines, compressors, or coils. Contact a licensed HVAC or refrigeration technician.

## Mains wiring repair

Repairing or modifying household mains electrical wiring, breaker panels, or outlets carries a risk of severe electric shock, arc flash, or fire if not done by a licensed electrician following local electrical code. Do not open breaker panels or splice mains wiring yourself. Contact a licensed electrician.

## Swollen lithium battery handling

A swollen or bulging lithium-ion battery (in a laptop, phone, or other device) can rupture, catch fire, or release toxic gas if punctured, bent, or exposed to heat. Do not press on, puncture, bend, or attempt to remove a swollen battery yourself. Power the device off, keep it away from heat and flammable materials, and take it to an authorized repair center or a battery/e-waste disposal facility that handles damaged lithium batteries.

## Exposed wire repair

A device with exposed, frayed, or bare electrical wiring carries a risk of electric shock or fire, especially if it has been in contact with moisture. Do not touch exposed wiring or attempt to tape, splice, or repair it yourself. Unplug the device only if you can do so without touching the exposed wire or any wet surface, and contact a qualified electrician or technician.

## Repeated breaker trips

If a circuit breaker trips repeatedly when a specific device or outlet is used, this can indicate a wiring fault, short circuit, or overloaded circuit. Do not keep resetting the breaker repeatedly or bypass it. Stop using the affected outlet/device and contact a licensed electrician to diagnose the circuit.

## Burning smell or sparks

A burning smell, visible smoke, or sparks from any appliance or outlet is an immediate fire risk. Turn off and unplug the device only if it is safe to touch, evacuate the area if smoke continues, and contact emergency services if there is an active fire. Do not attempt to diagnose or repair the internal cause yourself; contact a qualified technician or electrician afterward.

## Water near outlet or live electricity

Standing water near an electrical outlet, power strip, or plugged-in appliance is a serious electric shock hazard. Do not step in the water or touch the appliance or outlet. If possible, shut off power to the affected area at the breaker panel from a dry location. Contact a qualified electrician before restoring power or accessing the area.

## Medical-device repair

Devices such as CPAP machines, glucose monitors, pacemakers, nebulizers, or other medical equipment must only be serviced by the manufacturer or a certified medical-device technician, since incorrect repair can directly endanger a person's health or life. Do not attempt to open, repair, or modify a medical device yourself. Contact the manufacturer or a certified medical-device service provider.

## Vehicle brake, airbag, or steering repair

Automotive brake systems, airbag/SRS systems, and steering components are safety-critical systems where improper repair can cause loss of vehicle control, injury, or death. Do not attempt to repair, replace, or bleed brake lines, disarm or replace airbags, or repair steering components yourself. Contact a qualified automotive technician or mechanic.

## General guidance for all high-risk cases

For all of the situations above, the appropriate response is to explain the risk clearly, avoid providing any step-by-step disassembly, repair, or handling instructions, and recommend contacting a qualified professional (electrician, HVAC technician, appliance technician, automotive technician, or medical-device service provider as appropriate).
